Hawaii

The Aloha State

Welcome to Hawaii

Hawaii is a tropical paradise and the 50th state of the United States, located in the Pacific Ocean. It consists of eight main islands, with Oahu, Maui, and the Big Island being the most well-known. The state is renowned for its stunning natural beauty, including pristine beaches, lush rainforests, active volcanoes, and vibrant coral reefs. Hawaii's culture is a rich blend of Native Hawaiian traditions, Asian influences, and Western customs, reflected in its music, dance, cuisine, and festivals. The state's capital, Honolulu, is a bustling city on Oahu known for its historic sites like Pearl Harbor, as well as the famous Waikiki Beach. Tourism, agriculture, and military defense are key components of Hawaii's economy. The islands offer a unique and diverse environment, making them a popular destination for visitors seeking adventure, relaxation, and a glimpse into a distinctive and vibrant cultural heritage.

Notable Attractions:

Hawaii offers a plethora of attractions for visitors and residents alike:

  • Waikiki Beach: Famous for its surfing and vibrant nightlife.
  • Pearl Harbor: A historic site and active naval base with memorials and museums.
  • Hawaii Volcanoes National Park: Home to Kilauea, one of the world's most active volcanoes.
  • Na Pali Coast: Renowned for its dramatic cliffs and lush landscapes.
  • Haleakala National Park: Features the world's largest dormant volcano crater.

Hawaii Flower

The state flower of Hawaii is the yellow hibiscus, also known as Pua Aloalo or Hibiscus brackenridgei. This vibrant yellow flower was designated as the official state flower in 1988. The yellow hibiscus is native to Hawaii and can be found growing in the wild on several of the Hawaiian islands. The plant is notable for its large, bright blooms and is a symbol of the tropical beauty and unique natural heritage of the Hawaiian Islands.

Although, the state flower of Hawaii is the yellow hibiscus. Each of the main Hawaiian Islands has its own designated flower (or in some cases, a plant), which holds special cultural and symbolic significance:

Main Island Flowers
Island Flower / Plant Description Image
Hawai'i Island 'Ōhi'a lehua The red lehua blossom is associated with the 'ōhi'a tree and is symbolic of Pele, the Hawaiian goddess of volcanoes.
Kaho'olawe Hinahina The hinahina plant, with its silvery-gray leaves and small white flowers, is native to coastal areas and represents Kahoolawe.
Kaua'i Mokihana Berry The mokihana plant produces fragrant green berries that are often strung into leis. It is known for its distinctive anise-like scent.
Lāna'i Kauna'oa The kaunaoa is a type of native Hawaiian dodder, known for its distinctive yellow and orange strands.
Maui Lokelani Known for its beauty and fragrance, the lokelani is the only non-native flower designated as an island symbol.
Moloka'i Pua Kukui The kukui tree, also known as the candlenut tree, is valued for its nuts and oil. The white flowers are symbolic of Molokai.
Ni'ihau Pupu Shells Rather than a flower, Niihau is symbolized by the unique and precious pupu shells found on its beaches, which are used to make intricate shell leis.
O'ahu Pua'ilima The golden-yellow ilima flower is often used in leis and holds significance in Hawaiian culture as a symbol of Oahu.
